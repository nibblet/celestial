## Full Text

### Scene 1: Waking Dust

The Martian wind didn’t howl-it whispered, dragging red dust in long, ribboned currents across the excavation field. Galen Voss stood at the edge of the south trench, boots buried ankle-deep in fine particulate. It clung to everything: mesh tarp, support struts, the knees of his suit. Even after weeks of excavation, it found new places to settle. Like memory. Like regret.

Below him, the buried form of Valkyrie-1 stretched outward in shadowed curvature. What they’d uncovered so far resembled no human spacecraft. Its skin shimmered faintly in the morning haze-not light, exactly, but the suggestion of it, as if it remembered illumination.

His body moved easily across the uneven terrain-rejuvenation therapy had seen to that-but the weight of decades still pressed against his thoughts. Chronologically in his eighties, his restored body belied the accumulated weight of decisions, missions, and memories that no treatment could reverse.

Galen’s visor pinged once. Vital norms. Routine diagnostics. He ignored them.

Across the site, crew members moved with quiet efficiency-survey drones blinked through their loops, mechanical arms extracted sediment in deliberate arcs. It should’ve felt like progress. Instead, it felt like reverence.

“You’re quiet this morning, Commander.”

Aven’s voice broke softly through the comm. She approached with her helmet off, red-blonde hair tied back, eyes narrowed against the wind. After nearly five decades together, she could read his silences like navigational charts-each pause and tension mapped to meaning.

“Listening,” Galen said.

“To what?”

He took a moment, then shrugged. “The way silence sounds when something’s about to break it.”

Aven didn’t respond, but she stood beside him for a while, watching the excavation. Below, the silver curve of the hull seemed to exhale as the dust rolled back, revealing another segment of seamless, unbroken material.

“It’s never made a sound,” she said. “Not even during deep-core drills.”

“That’s what bothers me.”

She glanced at him. “You want it to resist?”

“I want it to react.”

Behind his words was the unspoken fear: that maybe it never would-that this was just a relic, long dead. But that wasn’t what kept him up at night. What haunted Galen Voss was the opposite-that it was waiting.

As the sun caught the hull’s surface, Galen couldn’t shake the sensation of being watched-not by cameras or sensors, but by something that understood what it saw.

Aven handed him a slate. New heat maps. Subsurface geometry was beginning to stabilize into patterns. Curves. Conduits. Almost architectural.

He tapped one corner. “Here. What’s this ridge?”

“Could be a resonance vein. Or natural mineral echo. Lena thinks otherwise.”

“Vault,” Galen said, the word hanging between them-a term that carried weight beyond its definition, hinting at treasures or dangers locked away. The kind of word that changed missions.

He looked up as the morning sun breached low over the crater rim. The Valkyrie’s surface caught it in a way that looked intentional. Not reflection-response. Just for a second.

A chill climbed his spine. For just a moment, as the dust settled in new patterns across the hull, he thought he saw something like writing-curves and lines that reminded him of constellations, or perhaps wings.

Aven saw it too. “Still think it’s just alloy?”

“I think,” Galen said quietly, “it’s listening.”

But listening to what-and why now?

Above them, the air remained still, the interface lines blank. No alerts. No message.

Just the silence of something unseen.

Or something unseen choosing silence.

### Scene 2: The Quiet Weight

The command shelter wasn’t much more than a reinforced dome of carbon-weave and polyglass, half-buried in red grit. Inside, the air recyclers hummed with the patient constancy of machines that knew better than to complain. The filtered atmosphere carried the sterile, metallic scent of efficiency-ozone, purification chemicals, and the faint memory of Earth embedded in the recycled air.

Galen removed his helmet and placed it on the anchor mount beside the entry, exhaling into the silence.

The room’s only light came from a bank of display panels on the far wall-diagnostics, excavation telemetry, environmental drift. None of it offered what he was looking for.

He keyed open the crew manifest, swiping through personnel logs. Each entry was intact: Ruiz, Osei, Reyes, Tran, Revas. No anomalies, no health concerns. Everyone performing at or above expectations. It should have felt reassuring.

Instead, it made him feel unnecessary.

He’d spent his career making decisions based on what he could measure and verify. Now he found himself staring at readings that defied explanation, questioning not what they meant, but what they asked of him.

He paused over Thane’s profile, his grandson’s face both familiar and strange-a continuation of something Galen himself might never finish. Rejuvenated in his thirties, still more curious than cautious. Physical responses trending higher than baseline. Neural patterns showing unusual harmonic stability during proximity events.

Galen stared at the vitals for a moment longer, then shut the screen.

As he closed the crew manifest, the display flickered-just once, almost imperceptibly. A single line of data in the corner of the screen rearranged itself before settling back into standard format. He might have dismissed it as system lag, except for one detail: the momentary pattern had resembled the same triple-curve he’d seen on the vault’s surface.

He reached to flag it, but the anomaly was already gone. Just another ghost in the system. Or perhaps something listening.

It wasn’t the data. It was the silence behind it.

He moved to the center console and called up the new subsurface scans. The structure beneath Valkyrie’s lower hull had begun to take on regularity. Repeating bands. Arched chambers. Something like a descending spiral-if it was architecture at all.

He didn’t like guesses. But every logical explanation kept collapsing under the same weight: symmetry without purpose.

The old instinct in him-the military one-called it a staging chamber. A place to gather before entry.

But into what?

Behind him, wind howled briefly across the outer structure. The dome creaked, but held. Galen didn’t turn. He was staring at a section of the overlay labeled Zone Theta, just under the southern curve of the ship. A new thermal trace had appeared there two days ago. Weak, steady, and without variation.

Except it wasn’t background radiation. It pulsed.

He zoomed in, calibrating the phase-contrast scanner for better resolution. The pattern wasn’t mechanical. It had no logic. It simply… was.

“Why now?” he muttered. “Why not when they first found the hull?”

He reached for the slate and added a short annotation, then paused, stylus still against the screen.

Maybe it had been waiting for someone.

The thought came unbidden, like a memory with no source.

He closed the system, stood up, and looked out through the small port window. The excavation field stretched beyond sight-sand, mesh, labor, time. All aimed at a shape that refused to reveal itself.

He felt it again: the stillness before something shifts.

Not a threat. Not yet.

But not peace either.

### Scene 3: The Unseen Below

Thane crouched near the edge of the shaft, helmet off, letting the dry Martian air tug gently at his hair under the filtered canopy. The taste of oxygen-enriched atmosphere never quite masked the undertone of iron and static that defined Mars-a planet that smelled like old memories.

He wasn’t supposed to be down this far-not unsupervised-but Ruiz’s security net was offline in this quadrant, and Lena was still arguing with the deep-drill team over artifact layering protocols.

He told himself he was here to “cross-check perimeter scans.” The truth felt simpler: something had drawn him.

Below, the trench sloped toward what they’d labeled Zone Theta, though Thane preferred not to name it. Naming implied understanding. He didn’t understand this.

He descended three meters, boots pressing gently into the red substrate. Just beneath the surface, the hull of Valkyrie-1 gleamed like glass held under water. Not buried, not even resting-but waiting, like a door someone forgot to open.

He closed his eyes.

And felt it.

A warmth-not heat exactly, but the memory of it, like the way a stone holds sunlight after the sun is gone. The hum in his chest wasn’t sound-it was recognition, as if something ancient had just whispered his name in a language he’d never learned but somehow understood.

It pulsed once. Then again. He placed his gloved hand near the surface.

Nothing visible changed. But somewhere in his chest, the hum deepened. What startled him wasn’t fear-it was familiarity. Like finding a door you’ve opened a thousand times in dreams.

“Thane, come in.”

The voice startled him. Aven.

He keyed his wrist mic. “Yeah. I’m here.”

“You’re registering an elevated pulse. You alright?”

“Just… got a little close to the core,” he said. “Didn’t mean to drift.”

There was a pause. Then: “Pull back for now. Marco’s doing a systems sweep.”

He didn’t move. Not right away.

The pulse was fading now, but he had felt it-like being remembered by something you hadn’t met yet. His hand traced a pattern in the dust-unconscious, unplanned-that mirrored the faint lines he thought he’d seen beneath the surface. Three curves. A circle. Something like wings.

He straightened slowly and climbed back to the surface. Dust clung to his gloves, patterned with faint shimmer where he’d touched the hull. It was already vanishing, like it had never been there.

But he had felt it. And so, he suspected, had it.
